No Aggregate Score

With more than a third of indicators unscored — and entire pillars resting on one or zero data points — a single overall number would imply false precision. Below: what the available evidence says, pillar by pillar.

I. Flows 5 / 6

A critically narrow tax base (2.2% GDP) sits beneath a structure dominated by remittances and ODA — both carrying their own dependency risk. The debt picture is clean, but that reflects HIPC relief, not borrowing capacity.

II. Expenditure 3 / 3

Education and health spending are both near-zero on-budget. The one favourable reading — debt service — is favourable only because Somalia has almost nothing left to repay post-HIPC, not because fiscal space is strong.

III. Spending Effectiveness 0 / 1

No scored indicator. IMF reporting describes steady PFM reform (SFMIS, payroll integration, audit law) — but no current PEFA assessment exists to verify it against. This pillar is a data gap, not a weak score.

IV. Enabling Environment 2 / 7

Sparse, but what's visible is stark: a CPI of 9/100 and no operational financing framework. Most governance and business-climate indicators (WGI, B-Ready) are simply unmeasured for Somalia — this picture is incomplete, not just weak.

V. Fin. Market Diagnostics 7 / 8

The most complete pillar — and a coherent story: banks are well-capitalised and liquid (13.7% CAR, NPLs low), but a 300%+ deposit-to-loan ratio shows credit isn't reaching firms. A plumbing problem, not a capital problem.

⚠️

Post-HIPC Reform Momentum: Somalia reached HIPC Completion Point in December 2023 — clearing external debt and unlocking a deeper IMF ECF arrangement. The IMF Article IV (Nov 2024) confirms strong program performance, real GDP growth upgraded to 4.0%, NPLs healthy at 3.4%, and publication of the Medium-Term Revenue Roadmap 2024–2027. The binding constraint remains domestic revenue at ~2.1% of GDP, alongside heavy reliance on remittances (~20% of GDP) and grants.

IMF Art. IV Cross-Reference: Where IMF Article IV provides more recent or comprehensive data than the framework primary sources, this is flagged in-card with the 🏛 marker. Notable IMF/WDI divergences worth resolving in the next framework iteration: remittances (IMF 19.8% vs WDI 15.8%), public debt (IMF 6.4% vs framework 8.6%), and NPLs (IMF 3.4% vs framework 2.5%). National FfD/INFF Framework score (currently 1) likely understates progress given MTRR publication and NTP 2025–2029 preparation — recommend review.

Intended Use: This scorecard is a structured conversation starter for donor–government dialogue on FfD priorities. It is not an official government document. Data should be verified against primary sources before use in formal assessments.

Critical Context: Somalia is navigating a multi-front transition: post-HIPC fiscal rebuild, federal-state political settlement, ATMIS security transition (Jan 2025), Somali shilling reintroduction preparatory work, EAC accession discussions, and accelerating climate vulnerability. FfD dialogue should be framed around domestic revenue mobilisation, financial-sector deepening, and institutional capacity sequencing under the ECF programme.
